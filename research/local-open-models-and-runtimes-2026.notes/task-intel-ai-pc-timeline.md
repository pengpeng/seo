## Findings

1. **2023-12-14 — Core Ultra Series 1 / Meteor Lake:** Intel launched its first Core Ultra mobile family for ultrathin H- and U-class laptops, introducing the tiled Foveros client design, Intel 4 compute tile, Arc iGPU and first integrated client NPU; representative SKUs include Core Ultra 7 165H and flagship Core Ultra 9 185H (up to 16 CPU cores, Arc graphics, Intel AI Boost), while external LPDDR5/x or DDR5—not package-integrated memory—remained standard [1][2].
2. **2024-09-03 announcement, 2024-09-24 retail — Core Ultra Series 2 / Lunar Lake:** the 200V family targeted premium thin-and-light and Copilot+ PCs with 4P+4 LP-E cores, Xe2 Arc 130V/140V graphics, a 40+ TOPS-class NPU (up to 48 TOPS), and 16/32 GB LPDDR5X mounted on the processor package; Core Ultra 7 258V and Core Ultra 9 288V are representative SKUs [3][4][5].
3. **2024-10-10 desktop then 2025-01-06 mobile unveiling — Core Ultra Series 2 / Arrow Lake:** Series 2 is not one architecture, because Arrow Lake joined Lunar Lake under the same marketing generation, with 200H/U laptops available from February 2025 and 200HX enthusiast laptops in H1 2025; representative mobile SKUs are Core Ultra 9 285H and Core Ultra 9 285HX [6][7][8][9].
4. Arrow Lake mobile spans mainstream U, performance thin-and-light H and enthusiast/mobile-workstation HX form factors: 285H combines 6P+8E+2 LP-E cores, Arc 140T and a modest NPU, whereas 285HX scales to 8P+16E and is commonly paired with a discrete GPU, so “AI PC” here emphasizes heterogeneous CPU/GPU/NPU acceleration rather than Copilot+ eligibility [7][8][9][15].
5. **2025-10-09 architecture reveal, 2026-01-05 launch, 2026-01-27 retail — Core Ultra Series 3 / Panther Lake:** Intel’s first 18A client platform reunified the Series 2 low-power/performance split for mobile, spanning mainstream through premium gaming/creator designs with up to 16 CPU cores, 12 Xe3 cores and 50 NPU TOPS; Core Ultra X9 388H and X7 368H are representative high-end SKUs [10][11][12][16].
6. **2026-04-16 — non-Ultra Core Series 3 / Wildcat Lake:** this distinct value-mobile offshoot uses a simpler two-tile design derived from the Panther Lake generation, up to 2P+4 LP-E CPU cores, one or two Xe3 cores and generally a 15–17 TOPS NPU, with Core 7 360 and Core 3 304 representative of school, small-business and entry laptop systems [13][14][17].
7. A slide group titled **“SoC” is defensible only as a broad client-platform umbrella** because Intel itself calls Meteor/Lunar/Panther system-on-chip designs and integrates CPU, iGPU and NPU in every covered family, but the label is technically loose for Arrow Lake-H/HX—especially HX systems designed around discrete GPUs and broader platform I/O—so “Intel client SoCs / processors” is safer [1][3][7][10][16].
8. **“Unified memory” is not an accurate family-wide attribute:** only Lunar Lake 200V standardizes 16/32 GB LPDDR5X memory on package, Intel’s precise term is “memory on package,” and Meteor Lake, Arrow Lake, Panther Lake and Wildcat Lake support external/soldered or modular DDR5/LPDDR5X configurations even though their iGPUs use shared system memory [1][3][5][8][10][14][16][17].
9. The clean slide chronology is therefore **Meteor Lake / Series 1 (Dec. 2023) → Lunar Lake 200V plus Arrow Lake 200S/H/U/HX / Series 2 (Sep. 2024–H1 2025) → Panther Lake / Core Ultra Series 3 (Jan. 2026) → Wildcat Lake / non-Ultra Core Series 3 (Apr. 2026)**, explicitly showing that Series 2 and Series 3 each contain more than one codename or tier [1][4][6][7][11][13][17].

## Deep Read Notes

### [3] Intel Lunar Lake architecture fact sheet

- Intel describes Lunar Lake as two logic tiles joined with Foveros plus separate LPDDR5X **memory on package**, not as “unified memory.”
- The compute tile consolidates the P-cores, E-cores, Xe2 GPU, NPU 4 and IPU; the platform controller tile carries security and connectivity, making 200V the clearest conventional SoC in the timeline.
- Its positioning is unambiguously thin-and-light efficiency: package memory reduces latency and system power, while NPU 4 reaches up to 48 TOPS and the Xe2 GPU exceeds 60 TOPS in Intel’s architecture claims.

### [7] Intel CES 2025 client launch

- Intel’s own segmentation shows why “Series 2 = Lunar Lake” is incomplete: 200V is Lunar Lake, while 200H/U/HX are Arrow Lake mobile additions under the same Series 2 brand.
- Arrow Lake-H targets premium thin-and-light creation/gaming with up to 16 cores, Arc graphics and up to 99 platform TOPS; Arrow Lake-HX targets 55 W-class enthusiast systems with up to 24 cores, 48 PCIe lanes and a 13-TOPS NPU.
- Availability is staggered rather than a single launch date: H/U systems began in February 2025, while HX systems were promised for H1 2025.

### [10] Intel Panther Lake architecture announcement

- Intel explicitly calls Panther Lake the first client SoCs built on Intel 18A and describes a scalable multi-chiplet architecture across consumer, commercial, gaming and edge form factors.
- The design goal is stated as Lunar Lake-level efficiency plus Arrow Lake-class performance, explaining why Panther Lake replaces Series 2’s bifurcated mobile strategy.
- Intel claimed up to 16 CPU cores, 12 Xe cores and 180 aggregate platform TOPS at architecture reveal; the later launch specifies up to 50 NPU TOPS, so NPU and total-platform figures must not be conflated.

## Gaps and contrary claims

- **Contrary interpretation — “all are SoCs”:** Intel marketing and architecture material often uses “SoC” broadly for tiled client packages, but an engineer may reserve the term for packages integrating most platform-controller functions; under that stricter definition Lunar Lake and Panther Lake fit most cleanly, while Arrow Lake-HX plus an external chipset/dGPU is better called a processor platform [3][7][10][16].
- **Contrary interpretation — “unified memory”:** one can call any iGPU architecture that shares system RAM with the CPU a unified-memory architecture in a generic UMA sense, but that wording invites an Apple-style coherence/package-memory claim that Intel does not make across these products; use “shared system memory,” and use “on-package LPDDR5X” only for Lunar Lake [3][5][8][14][17].
- Intel uses “launch,” “introduced,” “pre-order,” “on-shelf” and ARK quarter dates differently: Lunar Lake was introduced Sept. 3 but sold Sept. 24, Panther Lake launched Jan. 5 but sold Jan. 27, and Arrow Lake mobile was unveiled Jan. 6 with H/U in February and HX only specified as H1 availability [4][7][11].
- “Core Ultra Series 3” maps to Panther Lake, but the April 2026 **non-Ultra** “Core Series 3” maps to Wildcat Lake; Intel says the latter is built on Panther Lake foundations, while independent reporting treats it as distinct, simpler silicon, so it should not be mislabeled Panther Lake in a codename column [13][14][17].
- NPU and iGPU TOPS vary by SKU and Intel frequently advertises aggregate “platform TOPS”; the slide should label the metric explicitly and avoid comparing Meteor Lake’s, Arrow Lake’s, Lunar Lake’s and Panther Lake’s totals as if they were NPU-only figures [1][4][7][11].
- Exact first retail dates for individual Arrow Lake-HX models and some Wildcat Lake OEM designs remain OEM-dependent; Intel ARK supplies quarter-level launch metadata but not a universal system street date [7][9][13][14].
